# PROJECT OSPREY — The Ultramarathon Blueprint
*Your Expert Coach system for training, fueling, and racing — trailhead to finish line*

This is the ultra edition of the OSPREY Expert Coach program — the same engine behind the cycling, swimming, and rowing plans, rebuilt for the mountains and the long dark hours. It turns four onboarding answers into a personalized, periodized plan that makes you durable and fast without breaking you. Written for the athlete: plain language, real efforts, zero fluff.

## 1. How the Expert Coach Program Works

Every OSPREY plan is built from four onboarding inputs.

| # | Onboarding Input | What it drives |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Experience & current mileage — years running, miles/week, sessions/week | Starting volume, progression rate, base vs. specificity emphasis |
| 2 | Goal race & terrain — distance (50k→100mi+), vert, technicality, altitude | Long-run design, vert/descent dosing, heat/altitude prep, cutoff math |
| 3 | Timeline to race day — weeks until target | Macrocycle structure (Base/Build/Peak/Taper) |
| 4 | Constraints & injury history — schedule, terrain access, past injuries | Weekly session count, strength design, load caps and red flags |

**One-line philosophy:** Build a huge aerobic base, teach the body to burn fuel and eat descents, train the gut and the mind, and arrive at the start line durable and rested.

## 2. Training Philosophy

Ultra is aerobic-dominant durability. Races last hours to days and are >95% aerobic — the winner is whoever slows down least. Polarized 80/20: ~80% of running easy (conversational), ~20% genuinely hard, very little in the mushy middle. Time-on-feet matters more than raw pace; the long run and the back-to-back are the engine.

| Macrocycle | Weeks out | Emphasis | Looks like |
|---|---|---|---|
| Base | Furthest | Aerobic volume + durability | Rising easy mileage, long runs, general strength |
| Build | Mid | Specificity — terrain, vert, fuel | Race-terrain long runs, back-to-backs, climbing/descending |
| Peak | Weeks before | Biggest specific stimulus | Longest B2Bs, race-sim days, night/heat practice |
| Taper | Final 2–3 wks | Freshness | Volume cut ~25/25/30% across the weeks, sharpness kept |

**Effort anchor / zones** — ultra runs on effort (RPE) and heart rate more than pace, because terrain scrambles pace. Re-test aerobic markers every 4–6 weeks. 3:1 loading: three build weeks, one recovery week.

| Zone | Name | Effort / feel | HR (approx) | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Recovery | Very easy, nose-breathing | <70% max | Recovery, technique, fat-burning |
| 2 | Endurance | Conversational all day | 70–80% max | Aerobic base — the bulk of ultra training |
| 3 | Steady/Marathon | Comfortably hard, short sentences | 80–87% max | Sustained-effort durability, climbs |
| 4 | Threshold | Hard, few words | 87–92% max | Lactate clearance, raise the ceiling |
| 5 | VO2 / Hills | Very hard, breathing | 92%+ max | Aerobic power, hill economy (used sparingly) |

Most weekly miles live in Zone 1–2. The hard 20% (mostly Z3–4, a little Z5) sharpens the top. Ultra rewards patience — going too hard on easy days is the most common mistake.

## 3. The Key Sessions

- **Long run:** the cornerstone — 2–5+ hrs Z2 on race-like terrain. Builds fat-burning, mental durability, gut tolerance. Progress time, not pace.
- **Back-to-back long runs:** big Saturday + big Sunday on tired legs — simulates the second half of the race without one monster run. The signature ultra session.
- **Vert & descents:** long climbs (power-hike practice — hiking is a skill) plus deliberate descending to condition quads for the eccentric pounding that wrecks unprepared runners late.
- **Threshold/steady:** cruise intervals (e.g. 3–5×8–12 min Z4) or tempo climbs — raises the ceiling so race effort feels easy.
- **Race-sim/night/heat:** dress-rehearsal days — race kit, race fuel, headlamp, heat — nothing new on race day.
- **Eccentric strength:** downhill-specific quad conditioning (see §5).

## 4. Form, Terrain Skills & Economy

| Skill | What good looks like |
|---|---|
| Uphill | Efficient power-hike with hands on quads; short quick steps; run only what's economical |
| Downhill | Relaxed, quick cadence, slight forward lean, light feet — practiced, not braked |
| Technical footing | Eyes ahead reading the trail, quick reactive steps, quiet ankles |
| Cadence | ~170–180 on runnable ground; shorten stride uphill, don't overstride down |

**Durability-first:** economy on trail is skill + strength, not muscling. Power-hiking steep grades is faster and cheaper than forcing a run. Descending skill is trainable and saves the quads for the finish.

## 5. Strength & Cross-Training

| Phase | Focus | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Base | Movement quality + general strength | Squats, lunges, deadlifts, step-ups, single-leg, core |
| Build | Max strength → eccentric/durability | Heavier squats/deadlifts, weighted step-downs, downhill reps |
| Peak | Power maintenance + resilience | Explosive low-rep, plyos, eccentric emphasis |
| Taper | Maintenance + activation | Light, snappy, low volume |

**Priorities:** eccentric quad strength (downhill armor), single-leg stability, hips/glutes, braced trunk, ankle/foot resilience. Strength training twice a week — even 20–30 minutes a session — is one of the highest-return injury insurances in ultra. Cross-train (bike/hike/pool) to carry aerobic load with less impact when the joints are grumbly.

## 6. Nutrition & Hydration

| Day type | Carb target | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Easy / recovery | 3–5 g/kg/day | Keep protein and veg high |
| Moderate volume | 5–7 g/kg/day | Everyday range |
| High volume / long-run days | 8–10 g/kg/day | Big aerobic days |
| Peak / race-sim | 10–12 g/kg/day | Heaviest blocks and race week |

**In-race carbs:** 60–90 g/hr, up to 100–120 g/hr with a trained gut (glucose:fructose ~1:0.8 to 2:1 to raise absorption). Train the gut in long runs — race intake is a skill. **Protein:** ~1.6–2.0 g/kg/day; include some in long-race feeding. **Hydration:** drink to thirst — do NOT overdrink. Sodium ~300–600+ mg/hr, ~800 mg per litre of sweat (more for salty/heavy sweaters, hot conditions). Overdrinking dilutes sodium → hyponatremia (<135 mmol/L), which is dangerous; thirst-guided drinking + adequate sodium protects against it. **Race day:** start topped-up, eat early and often before you're empty, favor familiar foods, mix real food and gels/drink on longer races. Nothing new on race day.

## 7. Race Preparation by Distance & Terrain

| Race | Demand | Training emphasis | Race strategy |
|---|---|---|---|
| 50k | Fast-ultra, near-marathon intensity | Strong aerobic base + some threshold, moderate long runs | Controlled start, steady effort, fuel from the gun |
| 50 mi | Durability + fueling | Big long runs, B2Bs, terrain specificity | Even/negative effort; walk breaks by plan; eat every 30–45 min |
| 100k | Deep endurance + problem-solving | High volume, night practice, aid-station rehearsal | Patience early; manage feet/stomach; relentless forward progress |
| 100 mi | Survival + management | Max B2Bs, night+sleep-low, crew/pacer plan | Ultra-conservative first half; troubleshoot early; keep eating |
| Mountain / high-vert | Climbing + descending + altitude | Vert, power-hiking, descents, altitude/heat prep | Hike the steeps, protect quads, respect cutoffs |

**Pacing shape:** even to slightly-negative effort. The classic ultra error is banking time early — the back half punishes it far more than in road racing. Manage effort on climbs, run the runnable, cruise the descents.

## 8. Taper & Race Day

Cut volume over the final 2–3 weeks (roughly ~25% / 25% / 30% reductions across the weeks) while keeping some intensity and race-terrain touches. Rest restores the legs; a little sharpness keeps them awake.

| Lever | Do | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Volume | Reduce progressively over 2–3 wks | Sheds accumulated fatigue |
| Intensity | Keep short quality touches | Maintains sharpness and feel |
| Frequency | Keep most runs, shorter | Preserves rhythm |
| Duration | 100-milers taper longest; 50k shortest | Bigger races shed more fatigue |

**Race day:** dial nutrition and gear in advance; run your own effort from the start; break the race into aid-station-to-aid-station chunks; keep eating and drinking on schedule; expect low patches and ride them out. Heat acclimation 10–14 days out if racing hot.

## 9. Staying Healthy — Load & Red Flags

The .01% difference is consecutive healthy weeks. Most ultra injuries are overuse from load spikes: progress mileage ≤~10%/week, take a recovery week every 3–4 weeks (3:1). STOP/caution signals: **bone stress** (localized, worsening, point-tender pain — stop and assess, don't run through); **tendon** (Achilles/patellar pain that worsens with load — reduce, address calf/quad strength and cadence); **systemic** (persistent fatigue, elevated resting HR, poor sleep, stalling fitness — take a down week); **GI distress** (train the gut, don't ignore it). **Load rules:** build gradually, alternate hard/easy, intensity OR volume — not both, prioritize sleep, calcium + vitamin D for bone.

## 10. Four Runner Profiles

- **First Ultra (road-marathoner stepping up, 3–4 runs/wk, goal 50k):** extend the long run, add trail time and hiking, keep it mostly easy; introduce fueling practice; conservative +10%/wk.
- **Intermediate 50-miler (4–5 runs/wk, some trail base):** B2B long runs, vert + descents, threshold to lift the ceiling, full 16-wk build, race-sim day, 2–3 wk taper.
- **Mountain 100k/100mi (experienced, 5–6 runs/wk):** high volume, big vert, night/heat/sleep-low practice, crew/pacer + aid-station rehearsal, eccentric strength non-negotiable.
- **Time-crunched masters (busy pro, 4 runs/wk):** quality long runs + cross-training for aerobic load, strength twice weekly, extra recovery, effort-based pacing, injury prehab first.
